### Post 2 — Wednesday, March 12
**Type:** Data point / Article share
**Theme:** Quarterly attorney reports are broken

**Copy:**

Your quarterly attorney report is lying to you.

Not maliciously. Structurally.

Here is what I mean:

Defense counsel self-reports on their own performance. They pick the format. They control the narrative. They decide what gets highlighted and what gets buried.

Then that report lands on the desk of a VP Claims or General Counsel who has 3,000 other matters to think about.

The result: you are making seven-figure decisions based on a document designed to avoid accountability.

We studied this pattern across multiple carrier portfolios. What we found:

- Budget overruns were buried in narrative updates 73% of the time
- Case assessments contradicted docket activity in 1 out of 4 matters
- Settlement recommendations came months after the optimal window closed

This is not a people problem. It is a systems problem. And it is costing carriers millions.

### Post 4 — Monday, March 17
**Type:** Story / Contrarian insight
**Theme:** Litigation management is dead

**Copy:**

I have been saying this for two years and people are starting to agree:

Litigation management is dead.

Not the function. The philosophy.

Litigation management assumes the job is to track cases, manage vendors, and produce reports. Keep things organized. Keep things visible.

That was fine when a carrier had 500 open matters and a team of 10 attorneys they had worked with for 20 years.

It is not fine when you have 5,000 matters, 200 defense firms, $40M in annual defense spend, and regulators asking harder questions every quarter.

The new standard is litigation intelligence. Not tracking what happened. Predicting what will happen. Not managing attorneys. Measuring them. Not reporting on spend. Controlling it.

The carriers who figured this out first are seeing 25% reductions in defense costs and 10% reductions in settlement amounts. Not over years. Over quarters.

Litigation management gave you a rearview mirror. Litigation intelligence gives you a steering wheel.

I wrote the full case for why the old model is dead. Link in comments.

### Post 5 — Wednesday, March 19
**Type:** Data point / Tactical insight
**Theme:** Defense spend leakage

**Copy:**

Here is a number that should bother every VP Claims in the country:

25%.

That is the average reduction in defense spend when carriers move from passive oversight to active litigation intelligence.

Not by hiring more people. Not by cutting rates. Not by squeezing panel firms.

By doing three things differently:

1. Measuring attorney performance by outcome, not by activity. Billable hours tell you nothing about whether counsel is winning.

2. Identifying resolution windows in real time. The difference between settling a case at month 8 vs. month 18 is not just time. It is six figures in defense fees that bought you nothing.

3. Connecting case-level data to portfolio-level decisions. One bad case is a problem. A pattern of bad cases with the same firm in the same jurisdiction is a strategic failure.

Most carriers have the data to do all three of these things today.

They just do not have the system to connect them.

That is the gap we built CaseGlide to close.

### Post 6 — Friday, March 21
**Type:** Insight / Newsletter preview
**Theme:** Nuclear verdicts and what carriers are getting wrong

**Copy:**

The nuclear verdict conversation in insurance is loud.

It is also mostly wrong.

Here is what I keep hearing: "Juries are out of control." "Social inflation is unstoppable." "We just have to price for it."

That is the litigation equivalent of saying "the weather is bad" and then standing in the rain.

Nuclear verdicts are not random. They follow patterns. Specific jurisdictions. Specific case types. Specific plaintiff strategies. And most critically, specific failures in defense preparation that compound over 18-24 months before trial.

The carriers getting hit hardest are not the ones with the worst cases. They are the ones who lack the intelligence to see the trajectory early enough to intervene.

By the time a case looks like a nuclear verdict risk, the damage is usually done. The depositions were mishandled. The expert was retained too late. The settlement window closed six months ago.

The fix is not better trial lawyers. It is better intelligence earlier in the lifecycle.

We are publishing a deep analysis on this next week. If you want the full breakdown on what the data actually shows about nuclear verdict patterns, follow along.
